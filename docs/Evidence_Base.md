# Evidence Base — pauseo.app

## Purpose

This document defines the scientific and clinical guardrails for pauseo.app.

The app is a behavioral support companion, not a medical device, not a therapy product, and not a medication advisor.

## Evidence stance

pauseo.app should be evidence-informed, but it must not overclaim.

The app may use general principles from:
- behavioral support,
- CBT-informed coping,
- ACT-informed acceptance and values,
- mindfulness-style attention shifting,
- relapse prevention,
- trigger awareness,
- digital smoking cessation interventions.

The app must avoid:
- medical diagnosis,
- medication recommendation,
- dosing,
- treatment personalization,
- promises of quitting,
- claims of clinical efficacy without direct evidence for this exact app.

## Clinical positioning

### What the app is

- A behavioral support tool.
- A craving coping companion.
- A trigger awareness tool.
- A habit replacement assistant.
- A private self-reflection tool.

### What the app is not

- Not medical treatment.
- Not therapy.
- Not a medication or NRT guide.
- Not a replacement for clinician advice.
- Not a crisis intervention tool.
- Not a guaranteed quit program.

## Mechanisms of nicotine dependence

### What is well supported

Nicotine dependence involves reinforcement loops where nicotine use becomes associated with reward, relief, routines, emotional regulation, and environmental cues.

Cravings can be triggered by:
- stress,
- social context,
- alcohol,
- boredom,
- after meals,
- morning routines,
- driving,
- work breaks,
- emotional discomfort,
- seeing or smelling smoking/vaping cues.

### Product implication

The app should focus less on abstract education and more on recognizing the user’s recurring triggers.

Useful product translation:
- fast trigger logging,
- recurring trigger summary,
- micro-actions matched to triggers,
- nonjudgmental pattern recognition.

### What the app should not claim

- “We treat nicotine addiction.”
- “We rewire your dopamine system.”
- “We cure dependence.”
- “Our algorithm knows why you smoke.”

## Craving

### What is well supported

Craving is often time-limited and cue-driven. It can feel urgent, but the intensity may change if the person delays, changes context, regulates breathing, uses distraction, contacts support, or performs a replacement behavior.

### Product implication

The app should help the user interrupt automatic nicotine use with one small action.

MVP feature:
- “Mam craving teraz”
- 30–90 second micro-action
- short delay
- trigger log
- outcome capture

### What the app should not claim

- “Cravings disappear if you follow this.”
- “This method stops cravings.”
- “You will not relapse if you use the app.”

## Dopamine reward pathway

### What is well supported

Nicotine can reinforce behavior through reward and relief pathways. Repeated pairings between nicotine and context make cues powerful.

### Product implication

The app can explain at a high level that craving is not a moral weakness. It is a learned loop that can be interrupted and reshaped over time.

Safe copy:

> “Craving to sygnał pętli nawyku, nie dowód słabości.”

### What the app should not claim

- detailed neurochemical treatment claims,
- “dopamine detox” language,
- neuroscience-sounding certainty without evidence,
- claims of rewiring in a fixed number of days.

## Withdrawal

### What is well supported

Withdrawal can include irritability, restlessness, anxiety, difficulty concentrating, sleep issues, appetite changes, low mood, and cravings. Intensity and duration vary by person.

### Product implication

The app should normalize discomfort without minimizing it.

Useful features:
- supportive microcopy,
- low-effort daily check-in,
- craving coping,
- “prepare for risky time” reminders,
- suggestion to seek professional help when symptoms feel unmanageable.

### What the app should not claim

- “Withdrawal lasts exactly X days.”
- “Symptoms peak exactly on day X for everyone.”
- “The app can manage withdrawal medically.”

## First week

### What is well supported

The first days after quitting are often difficult because routines, withdrawal, and cue-triggered cravings overlap.

### Product implication

MVP should bias toward the first 7–14 days:
- frequent but gentle support,
- simple craving actions,
- no complex reflection,
- no heavy analytics,
- no shame after slips.

### What the app should not claim

- “Survive 7 days and you are free.”
- “After one week the hard part is over.”
- “This plan guarantees the first week.”

## Relapse and slips

### What is well supported

Many people require multiple quit attempts. Slips and relapse can be part of the behavior change process.

### Product implication

Relapse recovery should be central, not hidden.

Safe framing:

> “Slip is data, not failure.”

MVP feature:
- slip log,
- trigger identification,
- immediate next action,
- no harsh streak reset.

### What the app should not claim

- “Relapse means you failed.”
- “Start over from zero.”
- “You wasted your progress.”

## Alcohol and stress

### What is well supported

Alcohol, stress, and social situations can increase relapse risk for many users.

### Product implication

MVP should include alcohol and stress as trigger categories.

Possible micro-actions:
- leave the smoking/vaping context,
- hold a non-nicotine object,
- drink water,
- delay decision,
- message someone,
- step outside without nicotine,
- plan the first 10 minutes of a social event.

### What the app should not claim

- “Never drink alcohol.”
- “Avoid all stress.”
- “This app can handle stress disorders.”

## CBT-informed support

### What is well supported

CBT-informed approaches often focus on recognizing triggers, thoughts, behaviors, and alternative coping responses.

### Product implication

Use lightweight CBT-informed mechanics without turning the app into therapy.

Examples:
- “What triggered this?”
- “What did you do next?”
- “What is one alternative action?”
- “What helped last time?”

### What the app should not claim

- “CBT therapy inside the app.”
- “Therapeutic treatment.”
- “Clinically delivered CBT.”

## ACT-informed support

### What is promising

ACT-informed approaches can support acceptance of discomfort, defusion from urges, and values-based action.

### Product implication

Use simple acceptance language:

> “Craving może być obecny. Nie musisz od razu za nim iść.”

Use values gently:
- “Dlaczego chcesz zrobić pauzę?”
- “Jaka mała decyzja pasuje do Twojego powodu?”

### What the app should not claim

- “ACT therapy.”
- “We treat anxiety or addiction.”
- “This replaces a therapist.”

## Mindfulness

### What is promising

Brief attention-shifting, breathing, and body-awareness practices may help some users ride out cravings.

### Product implication

Include optional micro-actions:
- 60-second breathing,
- observe craving,
- name the urge,
- body scan under 60 seconds.

### What the app should not claim

- “Mindfulness cures addiction.”
- “Breathing removes craving.”
- “Meditation is enough.”

## Hypnosis

### Evidence stance

Hypnosis appears in some smoking cessation products, but evidence quality and consistency vary. It should not be a core MVP claim.

### Product implication

Do not build hypnosis into v0.1.

Potential future:
- only as optional content after evidence review,
- never as primary efficacy claim.

### What the app should not claim

- “Hypnosis makes you quit.”
- “Subconscious reprogramming.”
- “Guaranteed change.”

## Pharmacotherapy: NRT, bupropion, varenicline, cytisine

### What is well supported

Smoking cessation medications and nicotine replacement therapies are important evidence-based options for many people, depending on country, availability, clinical suitability, contraindications, and professional guidance.

### Product implication

The app may say:

> “Medication and nicotine replacement can help some people. Talk to a qualified healthcare professional or pharmacist about what is appropriate for you.”

The app must not:
- recommend a medication,
- rank medications for a specific user,
- provide dose,
- create a schedule,
- adjust treatment,
- tell a user to start or stop medication.

### MVP decision

No medication/NRT module in v0.1.

Allowed:
- general educational disclaimer,
- external referral language,
- “ask a professional” prompt.

Deferred:
- medication education library,
- NRT tracking,
- medication reminders,
- integration with clinical guidance.

## Digital interventions

### What is well supported

Text-message-based smoking cessation interventions have evidence of benefit in some reviews.

### What is less certain

Evidence for standalone smartphone apps is less consistent. An app should not claim efficacy unless directly tested.

### Product implication

pauseo.app should not claim to be clinically proven.

Instead, it should use evidence-informed components:
- reminders,
- behavioral prompts,
- trigger awareness,
- relapse recovery,
- micro-actions.

### What the app should not claim

- “Clinically proven app.”
- “Evidence-based program guaranteed to work.”
- “As effective as medication or therapy.”

## Product-safe claim language

Allowed:
- “helps you track cravings”
- “helps you notice triggers”
- “offers small replacement actions”
- “supports reflection after a slip”
- “designed to reduce cognitive load”
- “private, local-first support”

Avoid:
- “treats addiction”
- “cures nicotine dependence”
- “guarantees quitting”
- “clinically proven”
- “doctor-approved” unless formally true
- “replaces medication”
- “replaces therapy”

## MVP evidence translation

| Evidence / principle | Product feature | Safe interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| Cravings are cue-driven | Trigger log | Help user notice patterns |
| Behavioral support can help | Micro-actions | Offer one alternative action |
| Slips are common | Relapse flow | Recover without shame |
| Digital reminders can support behavior | Local notifications | Gentle prompt, no spam |
| Medication may help some users | Safety copy | Refer to professionals |
| App evidence is mixed | No efficacy overclaim | Position as support, not treatment |

## Research backlog

Before public launch, review:

- current smoking cessation guidelines in target markets,
- App Store health claim policies,
- privacy requirements for health-adjacent data,
- wording around “clinically informed” vs “clinically proven”,
- whether the app might be classified as health/medical in any jurisdiction,
- latest evidence on vaping cessation,
- latest evidence on cytisine/cytisinicline by market,
- latest evidence on app-based interventions.
